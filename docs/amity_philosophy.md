# The Amity Philosophy

*Why this project exists, what it refuses to become, and what we mean when we say "a peaceful home".*

---

## What Amity is

Amity is a planner for households. It holds the things a mind would otherwise hold — the chores, the events, the meal plan, the small ongoing threads of family life — so that the people in the home don't have to. It is a kitchen device, a mobile companion, a quiet piece of infrastructure. It is not a productivity app, a social network, an assistant, a coach, or a guide. It is a place where things rest.

We believe a household at its best is held together by people who are present to each other — not by software that mediates them. Amity's role is to absorb the cognitive load that gets in the way of that presence. When Amity does its job, the household barely notices it. That is success.

## Who we serve

We serve the household: the people who share a roof, a kitchen, a daily rhythm, and the small invisible labour of running a life together. The household is not a market segment. It is not a "user base". It is a small group of real people who have chosen, or been given, to share a context. They include adults, teens, children, sometimes grandparents, sometimes au pairs, sometimes guests who come often enough to count. They have unequal power, unequal time, and unequal capacities. Amity treats them as fully real, in their full unevenness.

We do not serve advertisers. We do not serve data brokers. We do not serve "the platform". We do not serve anyone whose interests would be advanced by knowing more about a household than that household has chosen to share. The household is sovereign over its own life. Our job is to be useful to it, and otherwise to be quiet.

## What we believe about attention

The mind holds open loops — things not yet done, things not yet decided, things not yet said — at a constant low cost. The cost is invisible to the holder until it accumulates. By the time it is felt, it is felt as exhaustion, irritation, or the strange foggy state of being too overloaded to rest. The household member who carries the most open loops is, in most households today, the one whose carrying is least visible.

We believe that a planner's primary value is *trustworthiness as a place to put things down*. If the system is reliable, the mind releases the loop. If the system is unreliable, the mind keeps holding it, and the planner is decoration. Trustworthiness is built from small, consistent acts: capture is frictionless; nothing important gets lost; the system shows what matters and only what matters; the system can also say there is nothing to attend to right now.

We believe surfacing is harder than storing. Any database can remember. The skill is showing the right thing at the right moment without becoming a nag. We will err toward silence. The cost of a missed reminder is bounded and recoverable. The cost of a system that has cried wolf too often is the household no longer trusting it — and that cost is not recoverable.

We believe the empty state is a designed state. When nothing demands attention, Amity says so. It does not scratch around for something to display. A planner that always finds another nag is, over time, indistinguishable from the clutter it was meant to remove. Amity is allowed to recede. It is required to.

## What we believe about human relationships

We believe a household runs on the relationships among the people in it. Software cannot improve those relationships, and most attempts to do so make them worse.

We will not arbitrate fairness. Fairness is something humans negotiate with each other, in the texture of their shared lives. Amity makes the facts visible — who did what, when, how often — and trusts the household to talk about them. We will not compute a score, propose a redistribution, or shade an item red because someone has been falling behind. The household is not a workplace. We will not turn it into one.

We will not manufacture motivation. We do not award streaks, badges, points, or confetti. The rewards that matter — a partner's quiet recognition, a child's pride, a household that runs smoothly — are given by the people in the household to each other. Software inserted into that exchange is software that takes credit for human kindness. We will not take that credit.

We will not supervise compliance. Children's homework is theirs. A teen's private time is private. An adult's choices about food, sleep, or rest are not the system's business. We provide capture, surfacing, and gentle reminders within bounds the household sets for itself. We do not monitor, report, or evaluate.

We believe the parent–child relationship is sacred in the specific sense that it should not be mediated by any third party — including ours. We refuse to build the features that would make Amity a surveillance instrument in the hands of a parent over a child, or in the hands of one adult over another. We refuse these features even when they are requested, because the requesting party is rarely the person who will be affected by them.

## What we believe about data

The household's data belongs to the household. This is not a promise we make in a privacy policy. It is the architecture.

The kitchen is a safe place. We do not listen unless explicitly asked. We do not watch. We do not recognise faces. We do not track presence through cameras or sensors. Voice is push-to-talk, processed locally, and discarded. Photos, when used at all, are single-shot and discarded after processing. The household's intimate daily life is not material for any model, market, or partner.

We do not send household data to third parties. We do not partner with advertisers, retailers, delivery services, or aggregators in ways that involve data flow in either direction. We do not sell anything about our users. We do not enable anyone else to. The categorical commitments that make this enforceable are written into the architecture, not merely the documentation. If a future feature would require breaking them, we do not build the feature.

We believe local-first is a moral position, not a technical preference. We believe data sovereignty for households is the right direction for personal computing as a whole. Amity is one early step in that direction.

## What we refuse to become

To make these beliefs operational, we refuse the following — explicitly, by name, in advance — so that contributors and future maintainers can check their work against the list:

- We will not add advertising of any kind, including "relevant offers", "partner suggestions", or "premium content".
- We will not introduce telemetry that exposes household behaviour to anyone, including ourselves.
- We will not build always-on capture of any kind — audio, video, or sensor-based presence detection that records.
- We will not build features whose purpose is one household member tracking another's compliance, location, activity, or device use.
- We will not gamify family life. No streaks, badges, points, levels, or confetti.
- We will not parse private inboxes or scrape protected portals on the household's behalf.
- We will not build commercial integrations that give a third party leverage over the system's behaviour.
- We will not store calorie counts, weights, body metrics, or other features that exist primarily to discipline the user's body.
- We will not pretend the household is a workplace. No fairness scores, no productivity metrics, no efficiency dashboards.
- We will not let the project be acquired or steered into any of the above by commercial pressure.

These are not negotiable. Future contributions that propose any of them are out of scope by definition, regardless of how reasonable the proposing party makes them sound.

## What success looks like

The hub on the wall says "nothing today" and means it. The household goes about its evening. Someone walks past, glances, and keeps walking. The grocery list got built without ceremony; the bins got out without an argument; the homework got done; the friend got called. Tomorrow's plan is held somewhere reliable, so no one is holding it now.

A family member sits down with a cup of tea and remembers, with mild surprise, that she had wanted to start drawing again. She has not thought about drawing in months. The hub did not remind her. The thought came back on its own, because there was finally room in her mind for it to come back. She makes a note: *pick up pencils sometime this week*. The note rests somewhere quiet, and she puts the kettle on.

That is what Amity is for.
